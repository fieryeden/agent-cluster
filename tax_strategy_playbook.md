# Tax Strategy Playbook for Litigation Finance Investors

Litigation finance—where capital is deployed on a non-recourse basis contingent on the successful resolution of legal claims—presents unique tax planning opportunities and challenges. Because returns are derived from litigation proceeds rather than traditional business operations or securities, the tax treatment is highly nuanced. 

This playbook outlines critical tax strategies for litigation finance investors, from entity structuring to harvesting losses.

---

## 1. Tax Classification: Capital Gains vs. Ordinary Income

The classification of litigation proceeds is the most contested and consequential tax issue for litigation finance investors.

*   **The Asset vs. Services Debate:** If the investor is viewed as purchasing an **asset** (an economic interest in the claim), the proceeds generally qualify for capital gains treatment (often long-term if the litigation exceeds one year). If the return is viewed as compensation for **services** or a substitute for the law firm's contingency fee, it is taxed as ordinary income.
*   **Non-Recourse Capital is Key:** Because litigation funding is typically non-recourse—meaning the investor can only recover from the proceeds of the successful case and has no right to pursue the claimant's other assets—the investment strongly resembles the purchase of a property right. This supports capital gains treatment.
*   **Passive vs. Active Involvement:** Investors must avoid taking an active role in the litigation strategy or case management. Active involvement risks the IRS reclassifying the investor as a partner providing services, which would trigger ordinary income rates (up to 37%) and self-employment taxes.

## 2. Timing Strategies: Fiscal Year Planning & Installment Sales

Litigation timelines are inherently unpredictable, often stretching over several years. This volatility requires proactive timing strategies.

*   **Fiscal Year Planning:** Pass-through entities (partnerships/S-corps) generally must use a calendar tax year. However, if a fund is structured as a C-corp, it can elect a fiscal year (e.g., ending June 30). This can defer tax liability by up to 11 months, allowing the fund to align tax payments with the actual receipt of litigation proceeds.
*   **Installment Sales (IRC §453):** When a case settles, the defendant often pays the award over time (structured settlements). If the investor receives proceeds over multiple years, they can elect the installment method, recognizing gain only as payments are received. This prevents a massive, one-year tax spike and aligns tax liability with cash flow.
*   **Constructive Receipt:** Investors must be careful not to trigger taxable income before they have actual cash in hand. If a settlement fund is established and the investor has the unrestricted right to withdraw funds, the IRS may assert constructive receipt, forcing immediate tax recognition.

## 3. Entity Structuring: LP/LLC vs. C-Corp & Carried Interest

Choosing the right entity dictates how returns are taxed and how profits flow to managers and investors.

*   **LP/LLC Pass-Through Structures:** The vast majority of litigation finance funds are structured as LPs or LLCs taxed as partnerships. This avoids the double taxation of C-corps and allows for flexible allocations of cash flow and tax attributes among investors.
*   **C-Corp Considerations:** While double taxation (21% corporate + dividend tax) is generally undesirable, C-corps may be used for foreign investors (to block Effectively Connected Income) or to take advantage of the 21% flat rate if ordinary income is a risk.
*   **Carried Interest Rules (IRC §1061):** The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) extended the holding period for carried interest to qualify for long-term capital gains rates from one year to **three years**. Because litigation routinely takes longer than three years, litigation finance managers often naturally meet this threshold. However, managers must carefully track the holding period from the date the fund acquires the litigation asset to the date of resolution.

## 4. Deduction Opportunities: Investment Expenses, Legal Costs & Management Fees

The TCJA severely limited miscellaneous itemized deductions, making it critical to structure costs as "above-the-line" deductions.

*   **Investment Expenses (IRC §212):** Expenses for the production of income are currently suspended for individual taxpayers (through 2025). However, if the investor is a "trader" in litigation claims (making frequent, continuous trades) or operates through a pass-through entity that elects to treat these as business expenses, they may be deductible.
*   **Legal Costs:** If funding is structured as a loan, **there is no immediate tax deduction** because loan disbursements are not deductible under IRC §162. However, if the funder pays legal costs directly as an equity investment in the claim, and the case is lost, the invested capital becomes a worthless security or a bad debt, allowing for a capital loss deduction.
