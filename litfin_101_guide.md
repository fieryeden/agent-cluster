# Litigation Financing 101: A Comprehensive Guide for Investors

## 1. What is Litigation Financing?
Litigation financing (also known as legal financing or third-party funding) is the practice where a specialized entity provides capital to a plaintiff or law firm to pursue a legal claim. In exchange, the funder receives a contingent share of the proceeds if the case is successful. 

Key characteristics include:
*   **Non-Recourse Funding:** The capital provided is non-recourse, meaning it is not traditional debt. The investment and return are paid *only* out of the proceeds generated from a successful resolution. If the case is lost, the funder loses their capital and the claimant owes nothing further.
*   **Third-Party Capital:** The funding comes from an outside entity that has no original stake in the underlying dispute, removing the financial burden from the litigant or law firm.
*   **Asset Class Trait:** Because the funder’s return is entirely dependent on the case outcome, litigation finance is treated as an alternative investment asset class rather than a traditional loan. For tax purposes, because these costs are treated as loans/non-recourse advances, they are not immediately deductible under IRC § 162 at the time they are paid by the firm; instead, the tax implications are recognized upon the resolution of the case.

## 2. Market Overview
Litigation finance has rapidly evolved from a niche concept into a mainstream alternative asset class. 
*   **Industry Size:** The global litigation finance market is currently valued at over **$17 billion**.
*   **Growth Rate:** The industry is experiencing explosive growth, boasting a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of **20%+**. 
*   **Growth Drivers:** This expansion is fueled by rising legal costs, corporate budget constraints, the institutionalization of the asset class, and an increasing acceptance of third-party funding in major jurisdictions globally.

## 3. Funding Structures
Investors can gain exposure to litigation finance through several distinct structures, each with varying risk/return profiles:

*   **Single-Case Funding:** Capital is deployed into one specific lawsuit. This offers the highest potential returns but also carries the highest concentration risk, as a single adverse ruling can wipe out the investment.
*   **Portfolio Funding:** Capital is provided to a law firm or plaintiff to finance a basket of multiple, unrelated cases. This provides built-in diversification, as the overall return relies on the aggregate success of the portfolio rather than a single outcome.
*   **Direct Equity:** Investors buy shares directly in publicly traded litigation finance companies, offering maximum liquidity but exposing the investor to the broader operational performance and public market volatility of the firm.
*   **Fund-of-Funds:** Capital is invested into a fund that subsequently allocates capital across multiple specialized litigation funds. This offers the highest level of diversification across case types, geographies, and fund managers, but includes an extra layer of management fees.

## 4. Return Profiles
Litigation finance offers return profiles that are largely uncorrelated with traditional public markets (stocks, bonds, real estate), making it highly attractive for portfolio diversification.

*   **Multiples:** Successful investments typically yield a **2x to 4x** multiple on invested capital (MOIC).
*   **Internal Rate of Return (IRR):** Target net IRRs generally range from **20% to 40%**, reflecting the high risk and illiquid nature of the asset class.
*   **Duration:** Cases typically take **2 to 5 years** to resolve from the time of funding to settlement or judgment. 

## 5. Risk Factors
While the returns can be exceptional, investors must carefully weigh several unique risks inherent to legal outcomes:

*   **Case Loss (Credit Risk):** Because funding is non-recourse, if the funded case loses or is dismissed, the investor loses 100% of the deployed capital.
*   **Duration Uncertainty:** The legal system is inherently unpredictable. Delays in discovery, court backlogs, and appeals can extend the life of a case well beyond the 2-5 year target, severely dragging down the IRR even if the ultimate multiple is preserved.
*   **Regulatory Changes:** The industry faces ongoing scrutiny. Changes in legislation, court rulings regarding the enforceability of funding agreements, or mandatory disclosure laws could adversely impact the business model.
*   **Adverse Cost Exposure:** In some jurisdictions, if a case is lost, the opposing party may be awarded legal costs, which the funder may be liable for depending on the funding agreement and local laws.
