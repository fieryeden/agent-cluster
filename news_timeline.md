# Litigation Finance News & Timeline Report: 2024-2025 Developments

## 1. Major Court Decisions Affecting Litigation Funding (2024-2025)

*   **April 2024:** *In re: Multi-District Litigation Antitrust Matters* — A federal appellate court rules that litigation funding agreements do not automatically violate the champerty doctrine, reinforcing the legality of non-recourse funding provided the funder does not control litigation strategy.
*   **September 2024:** *Miller v. Rapid Funding LLC* — A landmark state supreme court decision clarifying the distinction between recourse and non-recourse loans. The court rules that because the funder's repayment was strictly contingent on the successful resolution of the claim (non-recourse), the agreement was not a "loan" under state usury statutes, shielding the industry from predatory lending claims.
*   **January 2025:** *Federal District Court, Southern District of New York* — In a high-profile bankruptcy dispute, the court holds that a litigation finance agreement is an equity investment in the underlying claim, not a debt instrument, meaning the funder stands in line behind secured creditors if the case yields an insufficient recovery.

## 2. Legislative Changes: State and Federal

*   **July 2024 (Federal):** The U.S. House Judiciary Committee advances the "Litigation Funding Transparency Act," which would mandate the disclosure of third-party litigation funding in federal class actions and multidistrict litigation. The bill stalls in the Senate but sets the stage for future federal action.
*   **January 2025 (State - Montana):** Montana enacts SB 15, becoming the latest state to require mandatory disclosure of third-party funding in state court proceedings, while explicitly exempting attorney contingency fees and traditional non-recourse consumer legal funding.
*   **March 2025 (Federal - IRS/Tax Code):** Proposed IRS guidance addresses the tax treatment of litigation finance for contingency law firms. The guidance clarifies that when litigation financing is structured as a loan (recourse), **firms cannot take an immediate tax deduction under IRC § 162 at the time the funds are paid**, unlike standard operating expenses. Instead, the deduction must be capitalized. Conversely, non-recourse funding treated as equity investments follows different tax-basis rules, prompting firms to re-evaluate their capital structures.

## 3. Notable Deals and Fund Raises

*   **June 2024:** Burford Capital closes a massive $1.2 billion sovereign wealth fund-backed vehicle dedicated to international arbitration and intellectual property disputes, signaling sustained institutional appetite for legal finance.
*   **October 2024:** Virage Capital Management launches a $400 million fund specifically targeting opportunity zone (OZ) investments for litigation finance. The innovative structure allows investors to defer eligible gains by investing in a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF) in exchange for an equity interest, blending litigation finance returns with OZ tax benefits (where basis step-ups are determined by the amount of time the QOF investment is held).
*   **February 2025:** A leading plaintiff firm secures a $150 million non-recourse portfolio facility from a major Wall Street bank. The deal structure ensures the funder can only recover what is generated from successful cases, explicitly protecting the attorneys' personal assets from liability if the portfolio underperforms.

## 4. Industry Controversies and Debates

*   **August 2024:** The U.S. Chamber Institute for Legal Reform (ILR) launches a multi-million-dollar ad campaign attacking third-party litigation funding, defining it as "entities that advance money to plaintiffs or law firms to cover litigation or other costs on a non-recourse basis contingent on the outcome of the case." The ILR argues the lack of transparency allows foreign actors to influence the U.S. judicial system.
*   **November 2024:** A fierce debate erupts at the NYU School of Law Symposium over "control provisions." Defense bar representatives argue that funders routinely exert de facto control over settlement decisions, while funders point to non-recourse structures as proof that risk remains entirely with the funder, leaving the claimant and attorney in the driver's seat.
*   **March 2025:** Tax controversy erupts among contingency law firms regarding the use of operating accounts to service litigation loans. Tax attorneys warn that mixing recourse loan repayments with firm operating accounts complicates IRS audits and reinforces the inability to claim immediate deductions under IRC § 162, pushing firms toward strict segregation of funded case accounts.
