# Regulatory & Legal Changes Report: Litigation Finance (2023–2026)

## Executive Summary
The litigation finance industry is undergoing a period of intense regulatory scrutiny and legislative transformation. Between 2023 and 2025, the sector has seen a aggressive push for mandatory disclosure at both the federal and state levels, ongoing debates over the application of ancient champerty doctrines to modern non-recourse capital, and significant international reforms. This report outlines the current regulatory landscape, recent court rulings, and emerging trends projected through 2026.

---

## 1. Federal Legislation: Proposed Bills Affecting Litigation Funding (2023–2025)
Federal lawmakers have intensified their focus on third-party litigation funding, primarily driven by national security and litigation transparency concerns.

*   **March 2023:** U.S. Representatives Mike Johnson and Darrell Issa introduce the **Disclose Act** (H.R. 1492), proposing an amendment to the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure (FRCP) to require the automatic disclosure of any third-party litigation funding agreement in federal civil cases.
*   **December 2023:** The **Litigation Funding Transparency Act** is introduced in the Senate (S. 3590), mandating the disclosure of litigation funding sources in federal class actions and multidistrict litigation (MDLs). 
*   **March 2024:** The **Protecting Our Courts from Foreign Manipulation Act** is introduced. This bipartisan bill targets sovereign wealth funds and foreign state-owned entities, requiring mandatory disclosure and empowering the Attorney General to block foreign funding of U.S. litigation if it poses a threat to national security or intellectual property.
*   **Projected 2025:** While comprehensive federal disclosure bills have historically stalled, the national security framing of the 2024 bills gives them a higher probability of passage or executive action in 2025.

## 2. State-Level Rules: Disclosure and Registration Laws
State legislatures are bypassing federal gridlock by enacting their own disclosure and registration regimes, creating a patchwork of compliance requirements for funders.

*   **Vermont (VT):** 
    *   *May 2023:* Enacts H.B 115, becoming the first state to mandate consumer litigation finance registration. Funders must register with the Secretary of State and comply with specific disclosure requirements in advertising and contracts, akin to consumer lending laws.
*   **New York (NY):**
    *   *Ongoing 2023-2024:* NY Senate Bill S5788 proposes stringent licensing and registration requirements for consumer legal funding. While not yet enacted, it sets a baseline for future consumer-protection-focused legislation in the state.
*   **California (CA):**
    *   *January 2024:* AB 2825 is introduced, seeking to regulate consumer litigation funding under the California Financing Law, requiring funders to obtain a license from the Department of Financial Protection and Innovation.
*   **Florida (FL):**
    *   *March 2023:* Introduces SB 1276/HB 1189, proposing heavy disclosure requirements for all third-party funding in state courts and explicitly prohibiting foreign sovereign wealth funds from funding FL litigation. 
*   **Texas (TX):**
    *   *June 2023:* Enacts HB 19, creating the "Texas Business Court." While not explicitly a funding law, the business court's procedural rules are expected to adopt strict disclosure standards for funded litigation in commercial disputes by late 2025.

## 3. Court Disclosure Requirements: Federal Rules, State Rules, Model Rules
The debate over whether funded parties must disclose their financial backers to opposing counsel and the courts continues to evolve across procedural frameworks.

*   **Federal Rules (FRCP):** 
    *   *September 2023:* The Advisory Committee on Civil Rules declines to adopt a blanket disclosure amendment for litigation funding, citing a lack of empirical evidence of systemic abuse. However, the Committee encourages individual federal judges to utilize their broad case-management powers under FRCP 16 to order disclosure where conflicts of interest (e.g., funder control over settlement) are suspected.
*   **State Rules:**
    *   *2023–2024:* Wisconsin (Supreme Court Order 20-03) and West Virginia adopt local rules requiring automatic disclosure of third-party funding in certain state-level court proceedings.
*   **Model Rules:**
    *   *August 2023:* The Uniform Law Commission (ULC) finalizes the **Uniform Consumer Litigation Funding Act**, providing a model framework for states to regulate consumer funding (capping fees, requiring right-to-cancel, and mandating contract disclosures). Several state legislatures are expected to adopt this model in 2025.
